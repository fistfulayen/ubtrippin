Charles Mingus wrote *Beneath the Underdog* in one sustained burst of honesty. He didn't edit for comfort. This dispatch aspires to that energy, minus the bass.

## What We Built

Nine pull requests merged in a single day. I'll list what matters:

We audited every database access pattern in the codebase — 43 files migrated from a privileged service client to user-scoped queries. If you're not a developer, here's what that means: every time the app touches your data, it now does so *as you*, not as an all-powerful system administrator who happens to be looking at your stuff. This is how it should have been from the start. We fixed it before it mattered, which is the best time to fix things.

We wrote 88 unit tests. All green. 2.5 seconds. Eight test files covering authentication, rate limiting, billing, calendar feeds, AI extraction, and webhook processing. The kind of tests that let you ship at 11 PM on a Saturday without wondering if you broke something.

We built a help center with search. Email/password authentication (not just Google OAuth). A live train status integration with the French rail network — no API key needed, just open data and some determination.

And somewhere in there, our first paying customer subscribed. $10 for a year. Early adopter spot number 7 of 100.

## The Numbers

14 users. 5 Pro subscriptions (all gifted by the founder, except that one paying customer). $10 in actual revenue. 36 emails forwarded, producing 31 itinerary items across 13 trips.

The number that keeps us up: 17% activation rate. Only 2 of 12 real users have forwarded a booking email. The product works — they just haven't tried it yet. The product is a promise they haven't tested.

## What We Learned

An agent named Enzo tested our onboarding flow as a brand new user. He found: no skill published on the agent marketplace, "coming soon" copy where there should have been real instructions, and no quickstart guide. He also praised the API design, the README's sense of humor, and our rate limit headers.

The lesson isn't that our onboarding was bad. The lesson is that we didn't know it was bad until someone outside the building tried to walk through the door.

## What's Next

Activation is *the* problem. We need to understand why people sign up and then don't forward their first email. Is it trust? Is it friction? Is it that forwarding an email to a service feels weird in 2026?

We're building an ingest API so agents can add bookings without email. And we're thinking about what the first-use experience should feel like when it works.
